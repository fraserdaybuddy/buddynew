**Test Results Interpretation Guide**

What to Look For, What it Means, What Kills the Thesis

JOB-006 Sports Betting Intelligence System

March 2026 \| Covers Phase 1 Claims 1--3 + Phase 2 Backtest

Applies to: Darts (180s), Snooker (Centuries), Tennis (Aces)

1\. How to Read This Document

This guide tells you exactly what patterns to look for when you run each
test in the Phase 1 validation ladder. For every test, it defines what a
passing result looks like, what a failing result looks like, and what
ambiguous results mean. It also maps the cross-sport patterns you should
expect if the thesis holds --- and the warning signs that suggest you
are fooling yourself.

The document follows the same test sequence as the Model Strategy and
Analytics Testing Framework. Run the tests in order. Do not skip ahead.
A failure at any step must be diagnosed before proceeding.

> **Key Principle**
>
> You are looking for the SAME structural pattern across all three
> sports.
>
> If mismatch compression works in tennis but not darts, the thesis is
> sport-specific, not structural.
>
> A structural edge means the effect repeats wherever the
> opportunity/rate decomposition applies.

2\. Claim 1 --- Skill Gap Predicts Opportunity Count

This is the foundation. If skill gap does not predict how many units are
played, nothing else matters. You are testing whether bigger mismatches
produce shorter matches.

2.1 What You Are Measuring

For each sport, run a regression of units played against absolute skill
gap, controlling for format:

-   **Darts:** legs_played \~ \|avg_differential\| + format (BO11 / BO13
    / BO19)

-   **Snooker:** frames_played \~ \|frame_win_rate_diff\| + format (BO9
    / BO17 / BO35)

-   **Tennis:** service_games \~ \|serve_strength_diff\| + format (BO3 /
    BO5) + surface

2.2 Patterns That Confirm the Thesis

The Core Signal: Negative Slope

The coefficient on absolute_skill_gap must be negative and statistically
significant (p \< 0.05). This means: as the gap between players widens,
fewer units are played. This is the entire compression mechanism.

What the Scatter Plot Should Look Like

-   **Left side (small gaps):** Wide vertical spread. Close matches are
    unpredictable --- some go the distance, some don't. This variance is
    expected and healthy.

-   **Right side (large gaps):** Tighter cluster, shifted downward.
    Mismatches compress consistently. The ceiling drops. You should
    rarely see a 20-leg match when the average gap is 15+ points in
    darts.

-   **Shape:** A funnel that narrows and drops from left to right. Not a
    flat band, not a random cloud.

Cross-Sport Consistency

The single most important validation check: does the compression effect
appear in all three sports? You should see:

-   **Same direction:** Negative coefficient in all three regressions.
    No exceptions.

-   **Similar R² range:** Skill gap should explain 15--35% of variance
    in units played across all sports. If one sport shows R² \< 0.05,
    the thesis is weak there.

-   **Format interaction:** Longer formats amplify the effect. A
    mismatch in BO19 darts compresses by more absolute legs than the
    same mismatch in BO11. If longer formats show no additional
    compression, the model loses a key lever.

2.3 Patterns That Kill the Thesis

> **STOP Signal --- Claim 1 Failure**
>
> Flat or positive slope: gap has no relationship to units played, or
> larger gaps produce MORE units.
>
> This kills the entire model. Do not proceed to Claim 2. Investigate
> the skill gap metric itself.

-   **Flat slope (coefficient near zero):** Skill gap does not predict
    match length. The thesis assumes compression --- if it doesn't
    exist, there is no MISMATCH UNDER signal. Possible cause: your skill
    gap metric is poorly defined. Try a different proxy (Layer 2
    composite instead of Layer 1).

-   **Positive slope:** Bigger gaps produce LONGER matches. This would
    be bizarre but must be flagged. Likely cause: data error, reversed
    sign, or the gap metric is measuring something other than dominance.

-   **Works in one sport only:** If tennis shows clear compression but
    darts and snooker don't, the thesis is not structural --- it's a
    tennis-specific pattern. Pause the failing sports. Continue the
    passing sport alone but acknowledge the thesis is narrower than
    claimed.

2.4 Ambiguous Results --- What to Investigate

-   **Significant but weak (R² \< 0.10):** The effect exists but
    explains very little variance. This is borderline. The model may
    still work if the small effect is consistent and not priced by the
    market, but your edge will be thin and fragile. Proceed with caution
    --- tighten edge thresholds to 8%+ minimum.

-   **Significant only in long formats:** Compression only appears in
    BO17+/BO5 but not in short formats. This is actually plausible ---
    short formats have less room for compression. Restrict the model to
    long formats only and see if Claim 2 still holds.

-   **Non-linear relationship:** The effect kicks in only above a
    certain gap threshold (e.g., 12+ points in darts). Below that, no
    compression. This is fine --- it just means your MISMATCH_THRESHOLD
    needs to be set at that inflection point. Use a LOESS curve or
    segmented regression to find the knee.

3\. Claim 2 --- Units × Rate Beats Naive Average

This tests whether decomposing the prediction into two components
(expected units × event rate per unit) actually predicts totals better
than just using a player's season average total. If the decomposition
adds no value, the model is unnecessary complexity.

3.1 What You Are Comparing

For every match in your dataset, compute two predictions:

-   **Naive baseline:** Player A's season average total events + Player
    B's season average total events. No adjustment for opponent, format,
    or context.

-   **Model prediction:** Expected units played (from Claim 1
    regression) × each player's event rate per unit. Context-aware.

Then compare: which prediction has lower mean absolute error (MAE)
against actual outcomes?

3.2 Patterns That Confirm the Thesis

The Model Wins on MAE

The decomposed model should produce lower MAE than the naive average. By
how much matters:

Where the Model Should Win Most

The improvement should not be uniform. It should be largest in exactly
the scenarios your thesis predicts:

-   **High-mismatch matches:** This is where naive averages are most
    wrong. A player who averages 8 aces per match will produce far fewer
    against a dominant opponent in a 3-set blowout. The naive average
    misses this. Your model should capture it. If the MAE improvement
    disappears in mismatch matches, the decomposition is not doing its
    job.

-   **Long-format parity matches:** Two elite players in a BO5 or BO17+
    produce more units than average. The naive model underestimates
    totals here. Your model should catch the extended match driving
    extra events.

-   **Cross-surface in tennis:** A player who averages 10 aces overall
    but plays on grass (where aces spike) and clay (where they drop)
    should be better predicted by the surface-specific rate than the
    blended average.

Residual Pattern You Want to See

Plot residuals (actual − predicted) against skill gap. For the naive
model, residuals should be systematically negative at high gaps (it
overpredicts mismatches) and positive at low gaps (it underpredicts
parity). For your model, residuals should be centered around zero across
all gap levels --- the bias is removed.

3.3 Patterns That Kill the Thesis

> **STOP Signal --- Claim 2 Failure**
>
> Naive average beats the model, or ties it, across all segments.
>
> This means decomposition adds no predictive value. The model is
> pointless.

-   **Naive wins everywhere:** If season averages predict just as well,
    the market already accounts for match context implicitly. There is
    no informational edge in decomposing. This is a thesis failure, not
    a data failure.

-   **Model wins overall but loses on mismatches:** If the model only
    improves predictions on close matches (where both players play near
    their average anyway), the compression thesis specifically is not
    validated. The model is adding value through some other mechanism.

-   **Model has lower MAE but higher variance:** If the model's errors
    are smaller on average but more wildly distributed (higher standard
    deviation of residuals), it's unstable. A betting model needs
    consistent prediction, not occasional brilliance offset by
    occasional disasters.

3.4 Ambiguous Results

-   **Model wins in 2 of 3 sports:** This is workable. Proceed with the
    passing sports. Investigate why the third fails --- likely a data
    quality issue or a poorly calibrated skill gap metric for that
    sport.

-   **Model wins on UNDER predictions but not OVER:** The compression
    signal is real but the extension signal is not. This means MISMATCH
    UNDER bets are valid but PARITY OVER bets should be deprioritised or
    suspended until further evidence.

-   **Improvement varies wildly by time period:** Model wins strongly in
    2024 but not 2023. This suggests overfitting to recent patterns or a
    structural market change. Run a walk-forward split to check
    stability.

4\. Claim 3 --- Prediction Diverges from Market Line

This is the money question. Even if your model predicts well, the market
may already know everything you know. Claim 3 tests whether your model's
probability estimates diverge enough from market-implied probabilities
to generate a betting edge.

4.1 What You Are Measuring

For every match where you have both a model prediction and a real market
line:

-   **Model probability:** From your simulation, the percentage of runs
    where the total falls under (or over) the market line.

-   **Market implied probability:** Derived from the decimal odds. For
    odds of 1.85 on Under: implied_p = 1/1.85 = 54.1% (before vig
    removal).

-   **Edge:** model_p − market_implied_p. Positive = model thinks the
    outcome is more likely than the market does.

4.2 Patterns That Confirm the Thesis

Systematic Directional Divergence

You should see the model consistently assigning higher probabilities
than the market in specific, predictable scenarios:

-   **Mismatch matches:** Model p_under should be higher than market
    implied p_under. The market underestimates how much the match
    compresses. This is your primary edge source.

-   **Parity + long format:** Model p_over should be higher than market
    implied p_over. The market underestimates how much the match
    extends.

If these divergences are random (sometimes model is higher, sometimes
lower, with no pattern), there is no systematic edge.

Edge Distribution Shape

Plot a histogram of edge values across all matches. What you want to
see:

-   **Centered slightly above zero:** Most matches show a small positive
    edge (2--4%). The market is approximately efficient most of the
    time.

-   **Fat right tail:** A meaningful minority of matches (15--25%) show
    edges above 5%. These are your betting candidates. They should
    cluster in mismatch and parity scenarios.

-   **Thin left tail:** Very few matches where the model says the market
    is dramatically right and you are wrong. If the left tail is as fat
    as the right, the model is just noisy, not insightful.

4.3 Patterns That Kill the Thesis

> **STOP Signal --- Claim 3 Failure**
>
> No systematic divergence. Edge distribution centered at zero with no
> directional pattern.
>
> The market already prices what your model knows. There is no
> exploitable inefficiency.

-   **Edge is random noise:** If mismatch matches show the same average
    edge as random matches, the market already accounts for compression.
    Your model is correct but redundant.

-   **Edges only appear with synthetic lines:** If you pass Claim 3
    using population median as a proxy line but fail when real Betfair
    lines are introduced, the market is sharper than your proxy assumed.
    This is the most likely failure mode. Real market lines are set by
    sophisticated bettors who already model these dynamics.

-   **Edges are large but unprofitable when bet:** If edges of 10%+ show
    a win rate below 50%, the model's probability estimates are
    miscalibrated. The model thinks it sees value but its probability
    mapping is wrong. Go back to calibration.

4.4 The Synthetic Line Warning

> **Critical Caveat**
>
> If you are testing Claim 3 with population median as a synthetic line
> (because real Betfair data is not yet imported),
>
> any PASS is PROVISIONAL ONLY. You MUST re-test with real closing odds
> before proceeding to backtest.
>
> Real market lines are tighter. Expect 30--50% of synthetic-line edges
> to vanish against real odds.

5\. Cross-Sport Patterns --- Similarities, Trends, and Opposites

The thesis claims a structural pattern that repeats across sports. Here
is what structural consistency looks like versus sport-specific noise.

5.1 What Should Be the Same Across All Sports

Direction of Compression

In every sport, bigger skill gaps must produce fewer opportunity units.
If darts shows compression but snooker shows expansion under mismatch,
one of them is wrong. The direction must be unanimous.

Mismatch Underprediction by Markets

If markets underprice compression in tennis aces, they should also
underprice it in darts 180s and snooker centuries. The market blindspot
is structural: books focus on match winner odds and treat stat totals as
secondary. This blindspot should be consistent across sports.

Format Amplification

Longer formats should amplify both signals in all sports. BO19 darts
should show bigger compression than BO11. BO5 tennis should show more
parity extension than BO3. BO35 snooker should show more extreme effects
than BO9. If any sport shows the opposite (longer format = weaker
effect), investigate that sport's format data.

Stage Alignment

Early round mismatches should produce the clearest UNDER signals
(because early rounds have the widest skill gaps). Late-stage parity
should produce the clearest OVER signals (because only elite players
remain). This tournament structure effect should appear in all three
sports.

5.2 What Should Be Different Across Sports

Magnitude of Compression

The absolute size of the effect will differ. Tennis has more granular
units (service games vs legs vs frames) and more matches in the dataset.
Expect:

-   **Tennis:** Strongest statistical signal. Largest dataset. Most
    measurable compression. Most formats and surfaces to slice by.

-   **Darts:** Moderate signal. Fewer matches, but the 180s-per-leg rate
    is highly variable, which creates larger edges when the model is
    right.

-   **Snooker:** Weakest initial signal. Centuries are rare events
    (Poisson-like), so variance is inherently high. Small sample sizes
    per profile cell. Expect wider confidence intervals and lower
    statistical significance.

Which Signal Dominates

The balance between MISMATCH UNDER and PARITY OVER will differ by sport:

-   **Darts:** MISMATCH UNDER should dominate. Large average
    differentials in early rounds are common. Parity at the top is rarer
    because the PDC field has a steeper skill gradient.

-   **Tennis:** Both signals should be roughly balanced. Grand Slams
    produce deep parity (BO5, all top players present) and early-round
    mismatches (qualifiers vs seeds).

-   **Snooker:** PARITY OVER may be stronger. The WST tour is top-heavy
    --- many late-stage matches feature top 16 players, and longer
    formats (BO17, BO35) create extended century opportunities.

Surface and Venue Effects

Only tennis has surface as a variable. But all three sports have
venue/event effects:

-   **Tennis:** Grass amplifies aces per service game. Clay suppresses
    them. Expect the model to perform best on grass and worst on clay.

-   **Darts:** All matches on the same boards. No surface effect. But
    Premier League (weekly format, smaller field) may behave differently
    from World Championship (single elimination, huge field).

-   **Snooker:** The Crucible (World Championship) has famously
    different table conditions. Track whether Crucible results are an
    outlier.

The biggest risk in model building is not that the thesis fails --- it's
that you convince yourself it works when it doesn't. These are the
specific patterns that indicate self-deception.

6.2 Asymmetric Evidence Handling

-   **Explaining away failures:** If you find yourself adding caveats
    ("well, that tournament was unusual", "that player had an injury")
    to excuse failures but not applying the same scepticism to
    successes, you are cherry-picking. Apply the same exclusion criteria
    uniformly before looking at results.

-   **Optimising thresholds after seeing results:** If you set
    MISMATCH_THRESHOLD at 10 points, test, get a bad result, change it
    to 12, retest and get a good result --- that is curve fitting. Set
    thresholds BEFORE testing. If you must adjust, re-test on a held-out
    sample.

-   **Segment shopping:** If you slice the data by sport, format, stage,
    surface, year, and signal type, some segments will look profitable
    by random chance alone. With 50+ segments, expect 2--3 to show p \<
    0.05 purely by chance. Require the signal to appear in the aggregate
    first, then check segments for consistency.

6.3 Data Quality Masquerading as Signal

-   **Survivorship in form data:** If player form is only computed for
    players with 10+ matches, you are systematically excluding
    qualifiers and low-ranked players from mismatch analysis. These are
    exactly the matches where the thesis should work best. Check that
    your dataset includes the full range of skill gaps.

-   **Closing odds bias:** If you test against closing odds (final price
    before match starts), your model had access to the same information
    the market had at open. The real test is: does the model find edge
    at the time you would actually bet (usually hours before start)?
    Test against opening odds or morning-of odds where possible.

-   **NULL stats mishandled:** If NULL 180s or NULL centuries were
    accidentally treated as 0 during analysis, mismatch matches (where
    the weaker player often has missing data) will show artificially low
    totals. This creates a fake compression signal. Verify NULLs are
    excluded, never converted to zeros.

8\. Backtest Interpretation --- Phase 2 Results

Once Claims 1--3 pass, the backtest simulates actual betting decisions.
Here is how to read those results.

8.1 The Three Staking Profiles

Run all three profiles from the blueprint simultaneously. They answer
different questions:

-   **Conservative (flat £20):** Does the model pick winners? Removes
    staking noise entirely. If this loses money, the model's predictions
    are wrong regardless of how you size bets.

-   **Moderate (25% Kelly):** Is there positive expectation? Tests
    whether edge-based sizing adds value over flat staking. If
    conservative wins but moderate loses, Kelly is overreacting to noisy
    edge estimates.

-   **Conviction (full tiered Kelly):** Does confidence differentiation
    work? Tests whether VERY HIGH conviction bets outperform HIGH and
    MEDIUM. If all tiers perform equally, the confidence scoring system
    adds nothing.

8.2 What Healthy Backtest Results Look Like

-   **Equity curve:** Gradually rising with visible drawdowns. Should
    look like a bumpy upward slope, not a smooth line. A smooth equity
    curve in a 200-bet sample is a red flag for data leakage.

-   **Drawdown recovery:** Maximum drawdown between 10--20% of peak,
    recovering within 20--40 bets. If drawdown exceeds 25%, the model
    hit a structural regime it wasn't prepared for.

-   **Win rate by confidence tier:** VERY_HIGH should win more often
    than HIGH, which should win more than MEDIUM. If the ordering is
    random, confidence scoring is noise.

-   **Profit concentration:** Expect 60--70% of total profit to come
    from the top 20--30% of bets (highest edge). If profit is evenly
    distributed, the model isn't differentiating well between strong and
    weak setups.

8.3 Segmented Results You Must Check

Aggregate numbers hide structural problems. Always decompose:

-   **By direction (UNDER vs OVER):** If all profit comes from UNDER and
    OVER loses money, disable OVER. The signals may not be symmetric.

-   **By sport:** Each sport should be independently profitable or at
    worst breakeven. If one sport generates all losses, remove it.

-   **By year:** Results must be stable across 2023, 2024, 2025. If 2024
    carries all the profit, the model may be overfitting to a specific
    period.

-   **By format:** Long formats should show higher ROI than short
    formats (more room for the signal to express). If short formats
    outperform, the model may be capturing noise.

-   **By gap bucket:** The highest-gap bucket should show the best UNDER
    ROI. The lowest-gap bucket should show the best OVER ROI. If this
    ordering is jumbled, the skill gap classification isn't working.

8.4 Walk-Forward Stability

The walk-forward test splits time into train/test windows and
recalibrates between them. What to look for:

-   **Consistent sign:** Each test window should be individually
    profitable or close to zero. If one window loses 15% while others
    gain 10%, the model is fragile.

-   **Coefficients don't drift wildly:** If the k value in leg_win_prob
    jumps from 0.08 to 0.15 between recalibrations, the model is
    unstable. Coefficients should change by \< 20% between windows.

-   **Bet count doesn't collapse:** If the model finds 50 qualifying
    bets in Window 1 but only 8 in Window 2, the thresholds may be too
    tight or the market adapted. Check if lines shifted during that
    period.
